Implementation-grade contract

Codex-ready implementation spec for rebuilding babayara.com

Current through March 7, 2026. This document turns taste, evidence, conflicts, review pressure, and implementation constraints into a reproducible contract for a static GitHub Pages single-page site.

Scope

Static single-page HTML for GitHub Pages, local-first assets, no build step, semantic HTML, and public copy that behaves like an edited document rather than a process memo.

Truth model

Separate official records, live-site observations, local bundle state, and rendered observations. Do not blend them into one fiction.

Primary evidence

Official institutional sources, publisher pages, stable working-paper records, live-site inspection, and local HTML/renders, in that order when conflicts matter.

Executive summary

This document is the implementation-grade contract for rebuilding the academic site as a calm, static, GitHub-Pages-compatible single page. It was written after separating three realities that were previously being muddled together: official institutional records, the live public site as observed on 2026-03-07, and the editable local bundle. That separation matters because the live page, the official records, and the bundle do not agree on several high-risk facts. In particular, official Indiana University / Kelley material identifies Fahiz Baba-Yara as Assistant Professor of Finance and uses the institutional email `fababa@iu.edu` [E07][E08], while the live site still exposed a different email string and still carried an office block [E09]. Publisher pages also establish exact paper titles for the published Journal of Financial Economics and Review of Finance articles [E10][E11], while public working-paper records around the employee-news paper show title and coauthor drift that must be handled carefully rather than hidden [E12][E13].

The rebuild therefore follows a narrow doctrine: keep the page document-like, calm, legible, and semantically sturdy; keep the factual surface area small; keep the implementation boring in the best possible way; and move all process language, quality-assurance theatrics, and conflict management into supporting artifacts rather than the public copy. The resulting local site is a direct edit of the provided HTML bundle, not a speculative redesign from scratch. The user’s requested sentence was removed from the research section; the hero and teaching copy were tightened so that every sentence reads like something a person would actually mean; the mobile layout was adjusted to avoid the stray separator artifact; and a reduced-motion override was added so that smooth scrolling does not disrespect system preferences [E19][E20][E21][E30].

This spec is also a reproducibility document. It explains what changed, why it changed, how the evidence was weighed, which reviewer personas were used, what the debates surfaced, which uncertainties remain, and what a future coding model must preserve if it extends the page. It deliberately externalizes state into files: `context.json` for high-salience state, `rubric.md` for adaptive quality criteria, `evidence_ledger.md` for support, `sentence_register.json` for sentence-level review, the debate transcripts for adversarial critique, and the final `index.html` embedded verbatim for traceability. The site is not treated as complete until the public copy, the implementation, the evidence, and the review artifacts agree well enough that remaining uncertainty is narrow and explicitly disclosed.

Design north star

The design north star is compositional calm without affectation. “Calm” here is not a mood-board adjective. It is a measurable reduction in decision noise. A visitor landing on the page should immediately know where they are, who the scholar is, what fields they work in, how to access papers, and how to contact them at the institution level. Nothing in the first viewport should compete for attention merely because it can. The page should read more like an edited academic handout than a startup landing page. This is consistent with the user’s taste brief and with scan-oriented usability evidence showing that people on information-heavy pages often decide where to go next from structure and information scent rather than by reading every word in sequence [E32][E34].

The brief invoked faint Apple and Japanese influence, but this spec treats those influences as constraints on discipline rather than as aesthetic costumes. The relevant lessons are proportion, interval, restraint, and confidence in omission. The irrelevant lessons are glassy mimicry, chrome, faux-minimal emptiness, and decorative mysticism. In practical terms, that means whitespace must do structural work, asymmetry is allowed only when it increases legibility, and every repeated pattern must justify itself. The portrait exists to humanize the page and stabilize the hero composition; it does not get to bully the text column on mobile. The research list exists to lower retrieval costs for visitors and colleagues; it does not get to become a taxonomy museum or a procedural explanation of record reconciliation.

The north star also treats performance and accessibility as design materials rather than compliance afterthoughts. Semantic HTML is not merely cleaner code. It carries resilient defaults, clearer source order, better accessibility hooks, and less risk of interaction breakage when JavaScript is absent or disabled [E22][E31]. Explicit image dimensions reduce layout shifts [E29]. Respecting reduced-motion preferences is part of keeping the page calm for real bodies, not just for abstract users [E30]. Descriptive link labels improve information scent and scanning [E33][E34]. These points sound humble because they are humble, but humble decisions win on pages like this. The trick is not to build a little monument to web cleverness. The trick is to remove friction without making the site feel generic or machine-made.

Non-goals

The site is not trying to be a research portal, a course management system, a CV replacement, a publication database, or a personal-brand machine. It is not trying to maximize engagement, harvest attention, perform “innovation,” or advertise how minimal it is. It is not trying to display implementation virtue by bragging that it is static, JavaScript-light, or generated with assistance. It is not trying to visually cosplay any famous consumer brand, and it is not trying to sprinkle generic “Japanese-inspired” signifiers like seasoning over a perfectly ordinary layout. It is not trying to expose every bit of administrative detail available in public records. It is not trying to resolve every scholarly record conflict for the reader in prose. It is not trying to make the reviewer theater the main character.

These non-goals matter because many academic personal sites fail through accretion rather than through a single dramatic error. They become fragile because each small addition sounds defensible in isolation: a little control here, a little metadata badge there, a little explanatory note elsewhere, and suddenly the page behaves like a dashboard assembled by committee. The current live site demonstrates parts of that failure mode with its exposed office block, its older email string, its duplicate research identity drift, and its JS-dependent research controls [E09]. The bundle draft was already better, but it still contained copy that explained the site’s conflict-management process to ordinary visitors, which is exactly the kind of internal machinery the public page should not have to narrate.

Future extensions should therefore be judged first against subtraction: does the new thing remove uncertainty, duplication, or retrieval cost? Or does it merely add texture? The default answer should be “do less.” This does not mean the page should become skeletal or bloodless. It means that any future addition must earn its place by improving truthfulness, retrieval, or readability more than it increases page weight, maintenance burden, or interpretive noise.

Operational translation of the style brief

The operational translation of the style brief is a set of concrete page rules.

First, the page gets one real first act. The hero should establish identity, field, affiliation, and the main navigation targets without pretending to be a product launch. The first viewport should feel stable. That means restrained typography, ample but not wasteful vertical rhythm, and a visible hierarchy that survives scanning. The link cluster should avoid generic scent. Repeated visible labels like “PDF,” “Code,” and “SSRN” are acceptable only when their accessible names are expanded so that repeated controls do not become indistinguishable to assistive technology [E22][E33].

Second, the body should behave like a well-edited document. Section openings should be short and specific. Research entries should use a consistent internal grammar: title, authors, status / venue, short note only where necessary, then links. One visible canonical entry per project. When public records disagree on title or coauthor line, the visible page should still show a single canonical entry and, only if the disagreement matters to a reader, a concise note that states the drift plainly without making the page sound like a reconciliation report. The employee-news paper is the canonical example here: a March 2024 draft shows a three-author title about employee news consumption [E12], while another public repository record still presents an older uncertainty-focused title with a different coauthor line [E13]. The page should acknowledge that drift with one short note, not duplicate the project under multiple names.

Third, calm requires rhythm. That rhythm comes from margins, not from gimmicks. Headings should have enough space above them to act as section dividers. Entry spacing should be generous enough that individual projects do not fuse into a wall, but compact enough that the page does not become vertical fog. The metadata rail can be narrow, but not so narrow that dates and statuses look pinched. The mobile layout needs its own dignity rather than being a squashed desktop. In the local edit pass, the portrait was reduced on mobile and the separator bullets in the hero link row were removed under the small-screen breakpoint because the old arrangement produced an orphan dot and a fussy rhythm. That change is banal in code and meaningful in feel.

Fourth, public prose must sound intentional. The user explicitly requested that every sentence make sense. That requirement killed the process-heavy research-intro sentence because it forced visitors to read about the site’s list architecture rather than about the research itself. It also required tightening the teaching section, turning fragments into sentences where appropriate, and avoiding repetitive stems. Helen Sword’s “turgid prose and generic AI cadence” failure mode was built into the core debate for a reason: the danger is not just grammatical error; it is plausible-sounding emptiness. The final sentence set was therefore reviewed sentence by sentence by copy editors, finance professors, and financial magazine editors. Those reviews live in the reviewer files rather than on the public page.

Content architecture rules

The page architecture is intentionally shallow:

1. Hero. Identity, discipline, affiliation, and a compact cluster of key links. 2. Research. The main content block, with published and working papers in a single continuous list. No separate vanity architecture for “featured research” unless there is a hard evidence-backed reason. 3. Teaching. Lightweight routing to official course infrastructure. Avoid redundant restatements. 4. Short service / professional activity. Keep this brief and factual. 5. Contact and institutional linkage. Stay at institution level unless public records are stable enough to justify more specificity.

The architecture is single-page because it reduces navigation overhead and fits the content volume. GitHub Pages imposes static-hosting realities anyway, and the user’s goal is a calm, fast, reliable academic page rather than a mini CMS. However, single-page does not mean single-texture. Each section must have a distinct purpose, and sections should not steal jobs from each other. For example, the hero should not contain mini-biographies that belong in the body; the research section should not absorb administrative contact detail; the teaching section should not become a content silo when official course systems already exist.

Research is the structurally hardest section because academic records drift. Published papers have clean canonical anchors through publisher pages and DOIs [E10][E11]. Working papers do not. Some have SSRN records, some have university repository records, some are only visible on coauthor pages, and titles can evolve while status markers lag. The architecture rule here is brutal simplicity: one visible project identity per conceptual paper, one visible home in the list, and one concise note if public records meaningfully diverge. Duplicate visible entries are forbidden. Long conflict narratives belong in `evidence_ledger.md` and `codex_spec.html`, not in `index.html`.

The sentence register complements this architecture by ensuring that the public layer can be reviewed atomically. Each full sentence on the final page gets an ID, section tag, text string, source provenance, and note array. Headings and fragment labels are tracked separately rather than being forced into the sentence register. This matters because copy-editing a heading requires different criteria than copy-editing a full sentence. The review files then key off that register and record targeted comments rather than generalized vibes. That structure also keeps the active context lighter during prompt and response iterations because the model can work from a compact register instead of re-parsing the whole HTML each pass.

Copy standards to avoid AI-writing and AI-design giveaways

The copy standards are anti-generic by design. The public page must not sound like generated filler, a compliance memo, or a strangely self-conscious minimalist poem. To get there, the spec adopts several editorial rules.

Every sentence must have a job. If a sentence merely explains the site’s own machinery, it belongs in a support file unless that machinery materially helps a visitor interpret a fact. This is why the removed research-intro sentence failed. It described list governance, title drift, and note policy before telling the reader anything useful about the research. Its content was not false, but its placement was wrong. The replacement sentence, “Published and working papers are listed below,” is almost comically plain. That plainness is a feature. It clears the throat and gets out of the way.

Avoid repeated stems and mirrored structures that create a synthetic cadence. Human prose tolerates some asymmetry. It also tolerates occasional bluntness. The page should not be over-optimized into a polished smoothie. Tight writing in this context means concrete nouns, active syntax, and low-adjective density. Where the academic record requires nuance—especially around working-paper status or title drift—be explicit rather than euphemistic. “The entry below follows the March 2024 draft title; some public records still use an earlier title and coauthor line” is acceptable because it names the problem and stops. An elaborate explanation about canonicalization heuristics is not.

Link text must carry scent. Users scan. Generic link labels increase cognitive load and, when repeated, can become accessibility mud [E32][E33][E34]. Repeated visible short labels are acceptable only if accessible names expand them. For example, a visible label “PDF” may have an accessible name such as “PDF for Persistent and transitory components of firm characteristics.” This preserves the compact visual language while giving assistive technologies enough specificity. The same rule applies to “Code,” “Data,” “SSRN,” and similar repeated labels.

Fragments are allowed only when they are truly functioning as labels, not when they are masquerading as prose. One issue in the original bundle was that some public-facing fragments behaved like hidden memos. During the final edit pass, the hero lead and the refereeing line were turned into full sentences so that the page would not stumble into telegraphic awkwardness. The copy-review personas then checked sentence-by-sentence for ambiguity, academic overclaiming, dullness, rhythm, and factual leakage. Their comments are preserved in the family-specific review files so that future revisions can see not just what changed, but why.

Light and dark tokens

The visual system is intentionally modest. The implementation uses inline CSS in a single file for deployment simplicity, but the design logic still benefits from named tokens. The spec therefore defines a conceptual token set even if the shipped HTML encodes some values directly.

Light-mode tokens: - Background: warm off-white or very pale neutral, not clinical white. - Text: near-black with enough softness to avoid glare. - Secondary text: darker than many fashionable grays; quiet does not mean low contrast. - Accent: restrained ink or muted blue-black; no candy-color highlights. - Border/hairline: subtle neutral with enough contrast to frame without buzzing. - Focus ring: clearly visible, not decorative. Must satisfy WCAG 2.2 focus appearance expectations [E19][E21].

Dark-mode tokens are defined for future extension, not for forced immediate implementation. If added, they should preserve hierarchy rather than inverting every ratio. Many dark themes fail by making everything equally dim. The right move is to keep the reading surface calm, retain sufficient contrast, and avoid neon accents or high-gloss motifs. Because the current bundle is static and already stable in light mode, dark mode is a non-blocking extension rather than a ship criterion.

Spacing tokens should follow a restrained scale that supports editorial rhythm: tiny gaps for metadata pairs, small gaps within link clusters, medium gaps between intra-section blocks, large gaps between sections, and extra-large spacing above major section starts. The grid can permit asymmetry, but that asymmetry must be structural rather than ornamental. On mobile, spacing should tighten slightly while preserving section separation. The local edit pass already used this logic by shrinking the portrait and simplifying the hero-link separators to maintain rhythm.

Typography should favor native/system stacks for speed, resilience, and local-first loading. Avoid webfont dependencies, especially remote font CDNs, because they add failure modes and performance tax without meaningfully improving this page’s mission. Academic pages benefit from predictability more than typographic novelty. Line lengths should stay in a comfortable reading range, especially in the research list and paragraph text. This is part of “document-like calm”: the reader should not feel as though they are decoding a compressed poster.

Layout rhythm and component specifications

Hero component: The hero must contain the single H1, a short field statement, institution-level affiliation, portrait, and key links. Only one H1 is allowed in the document. The H1 should be the scholar’s name. The field statement should be a full sentence or a clean noun phrase that reads naturally; in the final local page it is a full sentence. The portrait must have explicit dimensions [E29], descriptive alt text if informative, and should not dominate mobile. Link controls in the hero must have generous target areas aligned with WCAG 2.2 minimum expectations [E20].

Research list component: Each item must expose title, author line, venue or status, optional note, and links. Titles should be sentence case where that is the published or working-paper convention; do not title-case everything into magazine headline soup. Author lines should respect official or widely cited public records. Venue / status text should be terse. Notes should only appear where conflict, versioning, or unusual availability matters. Duplicate visible entries are forbidden. If a paper is published, use the exact publisher title [E10][E11]. If a working paper’s title is current on multiple author pages, prefer that current title [E14][E15]. If records conflict meaningfully, show one title and one note, not multiple entries [E12][E13].

Teaching component: The teaching block should route users to official course materials or current course infrastructure with minimal redundancy. Academic visitors do not need the same instruction repeated in adjacent sentences. The local edit pass merged repetitive language for this reason. Keep the section factual and crisp.

Service / professional activity component: Use short prose or a compact list. Avoid self-congratulatory phrasing. If refereeing is mentioned, phrase it as a complete sentence or a clearly labeled fragment, but avoid awkward half-sentences.

Contact component: Prefer institution-level affiliation and verified institutional email. Do not expose office details unless public records are consistent and there is a reason ordinary visitors benefit from them. In this case official records themselves diverge from the live site on office details [E08][E09], so institution-level contact is safer. The contact surface should not imply availability beyond what the institution publicly supports.

Disclosure / note pattern: If the implementation uses disclosure widgets for additional notes, use native `<details>` and `<summary>` rather than custom JavaScript accordions where feasible [E23][E24]. The current shipped HTML prefers keeping notes inline and concise, which is even simpler. Any future disclosure should remain fully usable with JavaScript disabled.

Performance budgets

Performance budget philosophy: avoid invisible taxes. On a static academic page the budget should be conservative enough that regressions are obvious.

Critical-path budget: - Zero third-party JavaScript in the critical path [E28]. - Prefer zero executable JavaScript overall. - One HTML file plus local assets. - Images must declare width and height to minimize layout shift [E29]. - Avoid remote font loads, icon fonts, analytics bundles, and framework runtimes unless there is a compelling mission-driven reason.

Perceived-performance goals: - First viewport should be readable from HTML + CSS alone. - If images fail, the page should remain usable and intelligible. - If JavaScript is disabled, the page should still work fully for core tasks. - Link controls should not depend on client-side hydration or event listeners to reveal research information.

The current live site’s JS-dependent research controls are exactly the kind of hidden tax this rebuild is trying to avoid [E09]. They create fragility for limited gain. A static list with clear structure, semantic markup, and descriptive links is often faster in both network terms and human terms. This is not anti-interaction ideology; it is task-fit pragmatism. The site’s main job is to present stable academic information. Interactivity should only be introduced where it clearly reduces retrieval cost without compromising resilience.

Asset budgets should also preserve calm. Large images, looping background video, decorative parallax, and custom cursor nonsense all violate the mission. The portrait may be crisp, but it should be optimized and dimensioned. PDF links should remain direct rather than forcing a custom viewer. Any future additions—such as an optional publications filter—should be evaluated against an explicit “can this be accomplished in pure HTML?” question before introducing script.

Accessibility plan

Accessibility is not a decorative seal; it is the operational definition of whether the page remains usable when ideal assumptions fail. The plan centers on semantic HTML, keyboard reachability, focus visibility, descriptive naming, target sizing, motion respect, and source-order integrity.

Semantic HTML forms the base because it gives free structural clues to browsers and assistive technology [E22][E31]. Headings should form a sensible outline. The document must contain a single H1. Landmarks such as `header`, `main`, and `footer` are desirable where they fit the current structure. Lists should be real lists when the content is list-like. Repeated link labels need disambiguating accessible names. Images need dimensions and appropriate alternative text. If disclosures are used, native elements are preferred [E23][E24].

Keyboard and focus: Every interactive target must be reachable and visibly focused. WCAG 2.2 focus appearance guidance requires a minimum visible indicator area [E19][E21]. The site’s focus style should be obvious without becoming garish. It should not rely only on color shifts that are too subtle to notice. Because many academic sites quietly fail here, the spec treats focus styling as a required design element. Target sizes should align with the 24x24 CSS pixel minimum unless spacing exceptions apply [E20].

Motion and timing: Non-essential motion should be removable when the user prefers reduced motion [E30]. The local edit pass added a reduced-motion media query to neutralize smooth scrolling. This is a small change with outsized kindness.

Content clarity: Accessible content is also editorial content. Clear headings, concise section intros, and descriptive links help everyone, including screen-reader users and people scanning quickly [E32][E33][E34]. Jargon is acceptable where it is the discipline’s native language, but public contact and navigation copy should remain plain.

Testing expectations: A full accessibility audit would include keyboard-only traversal, inspection of heading structure, color-contrast checks, screen-reader spot checks, and mobile zoom sanity checks. In this task, the local page was visually rendered in a Chromium engine and inspected at desktop and mobile sizes, but linked resources were not exhaustively tested. That limitation must be kept visible. Honest limits beat fake certainty every time.

Metadata, SEO, Open Graph, and JSON-LD plan

Metadata and search discoverability on an academic personal site should be accurate, spare, and aligned with visible content. Search engines reward consistency more than decorative metadata overproduction. Google’s structured data guidance is explicit that structured data should truthfully represent what is visibly on the page [E25]. That means no hidden claims, no invisible affiliation flourishes, no paper statuses that exceed what the public page itself states, and no speculative JSON-LD expansions that look impressive but drift out of sync.

Minimum metadata plan: - Clear `<title>` with scholar name and role. - Concise meta description that accurately reflects the visible page. - Open Graph / social metadata if useful, but only if it mirrors visible identity and content. - Canonical URL for the domain in use. - Optional `Person` JSON-LD only if every key field is visible and verified. Because institutional details have some cross-source instability, the JSON-LD should stay conservative.

Discoverability is also a content-architecture issue. Publisher-canonical titles for published papers improve match quality [E10][E11]. Canonical single-entry handling for working papers reduces duplicate-entity confusion. Descriptive link labels help humans and can also improve the semantic clarity of outbound references. However, the page should not descend into SEO superstition. Search discoverability for an academic site depends far more on truthfulness, stable titles, and crawlable semantics than on metadata wizardry.

The spec therefore treats metadata as a supporting layer, not a performance art. If future maintainers cannot keep a metadata field synchronized with visible content, they should delete it rather than let it drift. The best metadata is the metadata you can keep honest.

GitHub Pages deployment notes

GitHub Pages deployment notes must remain modest because local files and a `CNAME` file do not prove the active production configuration. Official GitHub documentation explains that a `CNAME` file is created for branch-based publishing but is ignored by custom Actions workflows [E26], and troubleshooting guidance makes clear that an existing `CNAME` file alone does not prove the active Pages setup [E27]. In other words: a file named `CNAME` is a hint, not an oracle.

Deployment contract: - Static hosting only. - No build step required for the shipped page. - `index.html` and local assets must be sufficient. - Keep `CNAME` only if it still matches the intended canonical domain and if deployment practice actually uses it. - Do not claim successful deployment unless the live site is verified after publish.

This task edited the local bundle and produced an updated static site snapshot. It did not redeploy the site. That distinction is not bureaucratic. It protects against a common hallucination trap in build-and-review workflows where a model edits local files and then speaks as though the public internet has already changed. The spec explicitly forbids that leap. Future maintainers should verify the live domain after deployment, compare the live DOM against the shipped file, and confirm that custom-domain settings and TLS are correct in the actual GitHub Pages configuration rather than inferred from repository contents.

Security and privacy notes

Security and privacy for an academic static page are mainly about omission, restraint, and avoiding accidental disclosure. Troy Hunt’s persona in the core debate was included to guard precisely against the quiet habit of exposing more infrastructure or contact detail than the mission requires.

Privacy rule one: do not publish contact details simply because they exist somewhere public. Use the narrowest public contact surface that serves the page’s purpose. Here that means verified institutional email and institution-level affiliation, not office-room specificity, because public records are inconsistent and ordinary readers do not benefit from more detail [E08][E09].

Security rule two: avoid unnecessary script. Third-party JavaScript on static pages adds supply-chain risk, privacy leakage, and performance tax [E28]. The current implementation’s bias toward no executable JavaScript is therefore a security and privacy choice as much as a performance choice.

Security rule three: do not overstate deployment facts. Claims about domain configuration, analytics, or infrastructure must be verified from the live setup, not inferred from stray files or old repository state [E26][E27].

Content-integrity rule: when records conflict, disclose narrowly rather than fabricating resolution. In scholarly contexts, the privacy leak is not only personal information; it is also reputational confusion created by sloppy record handling. A single canonical entry with a short note protects both clarity and accuracy.

Future risk checks: - Confirm that external links use HTTPS and point to intended destinations. - Avoid embedding third-party trackers or social widgets. - Keep downloadable assets local where feasible. - Review any future contact additions through the question: does this materially help visitors, or does it merely widen the exposed surface?

Adaptive rubric

The adaptive rubric is the task’s steering wheel. It evolved across prompt and response iterations rather than being frozen up front. That self-improving behavior matters because the initial failures were not random; they revealed missing guardrails.

Prompt rubric dimensions: 1. Truth-model clarity: does the prompt clearly separate official records, live-site observations, local bundle state, and rendered observations? 2. Completion contract: does it say what counts as done? 3. Context compression: does it keep active context small and externalize state to files? 4. Direct-edit authority: does it explicitly permit editing the HTML when the user asked for an edit? 5. Reviewer staging: does it defer reviewer swarms until the draft is substantively stable? 6. Render honesty: does it distinguish browser-engine render, browser-path live render, alt-render, and screenshot inference?

Response rubric dimensions: 1. User request coverage. 2. Factual reliability. 3. Public-copy quality. 4. Semantic/accessibility resilience. 5. Performance restraint. 6. Visual calm. 7. Evidence-backed uncertainty handling. 8. Cross-file consistency. 9. Honesty about live versus local state. 10. Artifact completeness.

The rubric improved because the work improved. The first prompt rewrite still left ambiguity around direct edit authority and render claims; the second fixed that. The first response diagnosed issues but had not yet done the requested edit; the second fixed the page but still needed a final sentence-level pass; the third cleanly separated official, live, local, and rendered truths. Scores moved 74→89→96 for prompt quality and 84→92→96 for response quality, with response word counts growing 83→119→565 as the answer became more specific rather than more verbose for its own sake.

The important point is methodological: rubrics should not become decorative scoreboards. They should harden in response to concrete failure modes. When a failure recurs, the rubric must add a guardrail that would have caught it earlier next time. That is what happened here with the direct-edit rule, the render-truth taxonomy, and the explicit insistence that reviewer theater stay off the public page.

Decision log

The decision log is anchored in actual tensions rather than aesthetic whims.

Decision 1: separate truth streams. Rationale: official institutional records, live-site content, and the editable local bundle diverged on high-risk facts [E07][E08][E09]. Without explicit separation, the model would be tempted to produce a blended fiction.

Decision 2: remove the user-flagged research-intro sentence outright. Rationale: the sentence centered site process rather than reader need. It also made the research section sound like a conflict-management note. The replacement sentence is simpler and more truthful to the section’s function.

Decision 3: keep one canonical visible entry for the employee-news project. Rationale: public records show title/coauthor drift [E12][E13], but duplicate visible project entries increase confusion. A short note is cheaper than a duplicate architecture.

Decision 4: use official institutional email and institution-level affiliation. Rationale: official sources support `fababa@iu.edu` and the live site showed a different email [E07][E08][E09]. Office details were unstable across records, so they were not promoted.

Decision 5: preserve static simplicity and avoid introducing script. Rationale: the page’s mission does not justify JS-dependent controls; third-party and unnecessary script harm performance and resilience [E28].

Decision 6: adjust mobile composition. Rationale: local render inspection showed a stray separator bullet and a portrait that pressed too hard on the text rhythm. Small CSS changes substantially improved mobile calm.

Decision 7: add reduced-motion support. Rationale: smooth scrolling without a reduced-motion override conflicts with modern accessibility expectations [E30].

Decision 8: stage reviewer swarms after the page stabilized. Rationale: running 30+ personas too early bloats context and produces noise. Their value is highest after the draft is concrete.

Prompt refinement log

Prompt refinement occurred in two bounded passes.

Iteration 1 reframed the prompt around contracts rather than around sheer process abundance. The original prompt had ambition, but it also jammed live-site audit, local editing, reviewer orchestration, and packaging into one giant cognitive soup. The rewrite extracted a truth model, made files the explicit working memory, and contained reviewer swarms in later stages. This aligns with context-engineering practices such as write, select, compress, and isolate [E05][E06]. Score improved from 74 to 89 because the prompt became easier to execute faithfully.

Iteration 2 hardened the weak spots revealed by the first rewrite. It added a direct-edit rule so that explicit user edits would not be demoted into abstract diagnosis. It clarified render language so the model could not casually claim that it “rendered the draft from source” when only screenshots or local HTML existed. It tightened schemas, removed duplicated debate instructions, and sharpened completion criteria. Score improved from 89 to 96.

The final refined prompt therefore behaves less like an inspirational speech and more like a contract with a memory system. It tells the model what to externalize, what to verify, what counts as done, where to keep uncertainty, and how not to leak process language onto the public page. That is precisely the sort of prompt improvement OpenAI’s prompting guidance points toward: explicit instructions, structured outputs, clean completion criteria, and verification loops over vague exhortation [E01][E02][E03][E04].

Response iteration log

Response iteration 1 established the diagnosis: the live site and the bundle had to be treated as distinct evidence streams; the live page still exposed the wrong institutional email, an office block, and duplicate research identity drift; and the bundle still contained the specific sentence the user wanted removed. This was useful but incomplete.

Response iteration 2 performed the direct edits. The flagged sentence was removed. The hero lead became a full sentence. The teaching section lost redundant language. The metadata rail widened slightly. The employee-news note tightened. The mobile portrait shrank. Hero separator bullets disappeared under the small-screen breakpoint. A reduced-motion media query was added. This was the real turning point because the work stopped being diagnostic and became concrete.

Response iteration 3 integrated the final render findings and produced a current-state answer that explicitly distinguished four states: official records, live-site reality, edited local bundle, and rendered observations. This distinction is the answer’s backbone. Without it, any statement about the site risks silent equivocation. The final score landed at 96/100 because remaining uncertainty was mostly external to the local files: live deployment and office-detail drift across public records.

Word-count growth was not treated as a vanity metric. It simply reflected that a more grounded answer needs more nouns. The counts moved 83 → 119 → 565. That growth is healthy because each step added specificity rather than fluff.

All-personas crossfire summary

The 30-persona crossfire was noisy by design, but several themes converged.

Copy editors repeatedly pushed against sentence-level bloat, needless hedging, and mirrored phrasing. Their consensus was that the page improved once its prose stopped narrating its own governance. Professors focused less on elegance and more on epistemic hygiene: they objected to ambiguous working-paper status labels, duplicate project identities, and any line that could be read as overclaiming.
